

Agentic AI in Source-to-Pay: Use-Case Catalogue,
Controls, and a Pilot-to-Scale Roadmap
Executive summary
Agentic AI refers to AI systems that do more than generate text: they can pursue objectives through multi-
step planning, tool use, and action-taking in an environment (e.g., enterprise applications) with appropriate
feedback loops and guardrails. Recent research characterizes agentic AI as shifting AI from “conversation
partners” to “active collaborators” that can perceive context, reason, plan, act, and coordinate (including
multi-agent setups), while surfacing new risks such as “hallucination in action,” infinite loops, and prompt-
injection exposure as systems start executing real operations. 
In S2P, agentic AI’s value proposition is not just faster drafting/summarization; it is orchestrated autonomy
across cross-functional, exception-heavy workflows (intake → sourcing → contracting → procure-to-pay →
supplier performance/risk/analytics). Industry definitions of S2P suites emphasize an integrated lifecycle
across sourcing, contracting, requesting, procuring, receiving, and paying—typically modular, SaaS, and
ERP-agnostic—where   data   flows   across   upstream/downstream   artifacts   like   contracts,   POs,   receipts,
invoices, and payments. 
Across the S2P subprocesses you listed, the best near-term “agentic” pilots tend to cluster around high-
volume coordination and exception handling where (a) inputs are already digitally available, (b) decisions
can be bounded by policy, and (c) human approvals remain explicit. Examples supported by vendor
documentation   include   conversational   procurement   task   execution   and   navigation   (e.g.,   “complete
procurement tasks 50% faster” claims for embedded assistants), automated RFx bid analysis/supplier
response   summarization,   requisition-to-PO   assistance,   invoice   extraction/matching   with   touchless
processing targets, and process copilots that combine knowledge models, tool plans, and structured
outputs for orchestration and analytics. 
However, strong governance is essential. Gartner warns that over 40% of agentic AI projects may be
canceled by end of 2027, citing drivers such as escalating costs, unclear business value, and inadequate
risk   controls—and   highlights   “agent   washing”   (rebranding   assistants/RPA/chatbots   as   agents   without
substantive autonomy). This makes disciplined use-case selection, measurable KPIs, and auditable controls
non-negotiable. 
The   roadmap   in   this   report   prioritizes:   (1)   foundational   readiness   (data,   identity,   process   telemetry,
controls), (2) pilots that deliver measurable cycle-time/cost/accuracy improvements while keeping approvals
intact, and (3) progressive scaling toward multi-agent orchestration and closed-loop optimization only after
controls and auditability mature.

Assumptions and scope
This report assumes a cross-industry enterprise context with: (a) an existing ERP and/or S2P/P2P stack, (b)
meaningful transaction volumes (POs/invoices/sourcing events) and supplier base, and (c) standard control
requirements (segregation of duties, audit trails, privacy/security). These assumptions align with how S2P
suites are positioned as modular, ERP-agnostic solutions that integrate across the full process and require
robust APIs for adjacent systems. 
“Agentic AI” here is scoped to enterprise-grade agentic systems: LLM-orchestrated or hybrid agents that
can plan and use tools/APIs within predefined guardrails, rather than unconstrained autonomous systems.
This matches research emphasis on controllable orchestration and explicit guardrails as a pragmatic
response to reliability and safety risks when agents can take real actions. 
Maturity levels used in tables: -  Research: primarily academic/experimental; limited enterprise proof. -
Prototype:   vendor-announced,   preview,   or   early   adopter   deployments;   partial   proof.   -  Production:
generally available capabilities and/or multiple referenced deployments.
Implementation complexity is scored Low / Medium / High based on integration depth, data readiness
requirements, and control/audit workload, consistent with analyst cautions that integrating agents into
legacy systems can be technically complex and may require workflow redesign. 
What agentic AI is and what it can do in enterprise workflows
Academic and industry literature converges on a practical definition: agentic AI systems combine an LLM
“controller” with memory, planning/reflection, and tools/actions to pursue goals over multiple steps, often
with   feedback   and   possibly   with   multiple   collaborating   agents.   A   2026   architecture/taxonomy   paper
decomposes agents into components like Perception/Brain/Planning/Action/Tool Use/Collaboration and
explicitly highlights risks (prompt injection, hallucination in action) that become more severe when agents
execute operations rather than just produce text. 
Related foundational work illustrates key mechanisms that matter for S2P: - Reasoning + acting loops (e.g.,
ReAct)   show   how   interleaving   reasoning   traces   with   actions/tool   calls   can   reduce   hallucinations   by
grounding decisions in external sources and making trajectories more interpretable. 
-  Tool learning and tool selection (e.g., Toolformer) emphasizes that models can learn when and how to
call external APIs (calculator/search/etc.), a close analogue of calling ERP/S2P APIs for validation and
execution. 
-  Reflection and episodic memory (e.g., Reflexion) demonstrates improving agent behavior over repeated
trials by maintaining reflective memory, which maps to continuous improvement in exception handling
(e.g., invoice mismatch resolution patterns). 
A conceptual taxonomy further distinguishes “AI agents” (often modular, task-specific automation with
tools)   from   “agentic   AI”   (more   coordinated   autonomy   with   persistent   memory   and   multi-agent
collaboration), providing a useful lens for procurement leaders: many “AI features” in procurement are
assistant-like; true agentic behavior typically requires orchestration, policy constraints, statefulness, and
traceability.

For procurement specifically, industry research indicates both strong interest and early-stage deployment
realities. The Hackett Group reports that nearly two-thirds of procurement executives expect GenAI to
fundamentally change operations within five years and notes early pilots and embedded features in areas
like PO processing, spend analytics, and e-procurement—while also emphasizing data quality and risk
concerns. 
Source-to-pay process model and reference architecture for agents
An authoritative market definition frames S2P as an integrated solution set to source, contract, request,
procure, receive, and pay for goods/services across the enterprise, typically SaaS and modular, with data
flowing across related artifacts (contract ↔ PO ↔ receipts ↔ invoices ↔ payments).    This is the
architectural precondition for agentic AI: agents need  a coherent process graph  and  consistent data
semantics to act reliably.

Reference architecture for agentic AI in S2P
A pragmatic enterprise pattern is a guardrailed orchestration layer that sits alongside (not “inside”) core
systems: -  Agent  control  plane: policies, tool allow-lists, approval thresholds, logging, and evaluation/
monitoring—reflecting   research   emphasis   on   controllable   orchestration   and   the   need   to   prevent
“hallucination in action.” 
-   Tool  adapters: ERP/S2P/CLM APIs, document ingestion (IDP), supplier-risk/ESG feeds, and e-invoicing
networks. S2P suites themselves highlight the need for robust API access for real-time integration with
adjacent systems. 
-  Process telemetry: event logs and process intelligence; “process copilots” are increasingly described as

Observations and cross-subprocess patterns
The strongest evidence for production-grade agentic patterns today is concentrated where (a) workflows
already have structured data and (b) action space can be bounded: invoice ingestion/matching, guided
buying/intake, RFx summarization/bid analysis, and process copilots generating structured outputs for
downstream orchestration. 
In contrast, “full autonomy” in negotiation and payment is inherently higher risk because it can directly
affect   supplier   relationships   and   financial   controls;   even   documented   pilots   emphasize   relationship-
management challenges and the need to balance automation with human interaction.

Risks, governance, auditability, and regulatory considerations
Agentic AI changes risk posture because failures are no longer limited to incorrect text—they can become
incorrect actions. Research explicitly calls out “hallucination in action,” infinite loops, and prompt injection
as key open challenges when agents operate in open-ended environments and consume untrusted content.

Governance operating model
A governance design that withstands audit typically combines: - AI risk management lifecycle aligned to
NIST’s   GOVERN/MAP/MEASURE/MANAGE;   in   practice   this   means   defining   accountability   and   policies
(GOVERN), documenting intended use and contexts (MAP), measuring performance and risks (MEASURE),
and implementing mitigations and monitoring (MANAGE). 
-  Management system approach (ISO/IEC 42001) for organizational policies, risk treatment, and continual
improvement for AI-based systems. 
-  Audit-ready evidence: immutable logs of tool calls, inputs, outputs, approvals, and system state changes.
This is consistent with the EU AI Act’s emphasis on logging/traceability and documentation for regulated
classes of AI systems. 
-  Clarity on “what needs agentic AI”: Gartner’s caution about agent washing implies governance should
require a justification for autonomy level and why simpler automation/assistants are insufficient.

Prioritized roadmap from pilot to scale
Two constraints dominate successful scaling: (1) integration and data readiness and (2) operational risk
controls. This matches Gartner’s advice to pursue agentic AI only where it delivers clear value and to
recognize integration into legacy systems can be complex and costly.    It also aligns with APQC’s
observation that P2P bottlenecks persist when requests lack details and invoice information mismatches
stall payments—precisely the friction points agents can address if data and guardrails are strong.

Implementation notes that materially affect outcomes
First, data and process fragmentation are a primary blocker. SAP explicitly frames “AI only delivers value
when supported by the right data, processes, and platform architecture,” and Gartner similarly frames
integration and data-flow across S2P artifacts as key to visibility and execution. 
Second, treat agentic AI as a portfolio of autonomy levels, not a binary. Gartner explicitly recommends
using agents when decisions are needed, automation for routine workflows, and assistants for simple
retrieval—reinforcing a layered approach rather than forcing “agentic” everywhere. 
Third,   maximize   auditability   by   design.   Platforms   that   generate   structured   outputs   (schemas)   for
downstream orchestration reduce brittleness and improve repeatability, which is foundational for controls
and audit evidence in S2P.
